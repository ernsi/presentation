News & Analysis 01 article

US-Israeli strikes on Iran have abruptly halted what appeared to be a constructive start to 2026 for equity markets. Brent crude and WTI have surged past $100 per barrel, driven by acute supply disruptions through the Strait of Hormuz — a vital chokepoint through which roughly 20% of the world's crude oil transits annually. With investors increasingly pricing in the prospect of a protracted conflict and a sustained blockage of this critical shipping lane, the S&P 500 has shed almost 360 b.p., while the Dow Jones Industrial Average has fallen over 430 b.p. from recent highs.

Yet the geopolitical shock does not stand alone. February's nonfarm payrolls fell 92,000, well below the consensus expectation of a 55,000 gain, while the unemployment rate edged higher to 4.4%. The combination of rising unemployment, elevated energy prices, and an unstable geopolitical backdrop has prompted comparisons to the stagflation of the 1970s. The Fed now finds itself in a deeply uncomfortable position: cut rates to defend employment at the risk of entrenching inflation, or hold its policy steady to control prices at the cost of a sharper economic slowdown. Neither path is clean, and markets are beginning to price in that uncertainty.

On the equity side, the AI capex narrative which underpinned much of the bull case heading into 2026 is facing increased investor scrutiny. Concerns over the sustainability of data centre spending by hyperscalers and the Magnificent Seven have rippled across multiple sectors — from software and data providers to technology hardware manufacturers and financial institutions with sizeable exposure to technology lending. These developments cast doubt on the premise that AI-driven earnings growth would broaden beyond mega-cap technology into the wider S&P 500 in 2026.

That said, the bear case is not without its counterweights. The fundamentals have not broken. Household balance sheets are healthy, real PCE is expanding, and S&P 500 earnings are on track for 14% growth, modestly ahead of last year's 13%.

One might expect this environment to drive capital into traditional safe havens. Yet cross-asset correlations are offering little comfort. Both gold and US Treasuries closed lower last week even as volatility surged — a reminder that during periods of acute macro stress, conventional diversification relationships can break down precisely when they are needed most.

Looking ahead, the key variable to monitor is the persistence of the oil price shock. Should crude prices sustain levels above $100 per barrel, it would represent more than a transient geopolitical premium. It could signal the beginning of a structural shift in the global trade and energy landscape, with deep downstream consequences for inflation, monetary policy, and equity valuations across the board.
